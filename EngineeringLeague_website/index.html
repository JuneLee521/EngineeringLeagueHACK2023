Project Overview

When our team first came together, we all immediately agreed we wanted our gauntlet designed into two parts. This meant that the wrist could be allowed to move freely. We also wanted a design that was flexible according to how much progress we could accomplish in our allotted time. We focused on one sensor at a time, but if we could fit one more into our time frame, then on our gauntlet we would have the space and flexibility to do so. We also knew that in our final product, we wanted the sensors and other components to be on the hand separate from the battery and breadboard which would be placed on the arm portion.

"Godlet" Breakdown

Sensors:

As our hero embarks on their mission to save the Bruin engineers, our team chose each sensor specifically to help tackle the challenges they face ahead. As they make their way through the dark rooms, the ring LED placed at the palm of the gauntlet lights their path for easier navigation. When they unknowingly stumble onto a room that is filled with gas leaks, the hazardous gas detection will indicate the levels of intoxicating gas. The temperature and humidity sensor help with detecting if a fire is nearby as it lets the hero know the amount of water in the air and an increase in temperature levels. This is mounted onto a micro servo that extends from the gauntlet to allow distance between the danger and the hero. There is also a buzzer that sounds off to the hero when the levels from the temperature and hazardous gas sensor become too great. When the hero finally makes their way to try to locate the engineers, the ultrasonic sensor is used to locate how far away they are, if even present at all. With all of these sensors, and their readings easily broadcasted on the OLED screen, the hero can successfully complete their mission to save the Bruin engineers.

Code Breakdown

Code:

In our code, the very first problem we wanted to tackle was to connect the ESP32 to Wifi and be able to transmit our sensor readings onto the website. After establishing the connection, the next step was to set up each of our sensors. To each one we had the sensor read the incoming data and then display it on the OLED screen on our gauntlet and transmit it to mission control. After doing this generally for each sensor and component we were able to specifically code extra items into our design plan. We included two buttons. One to turn the ring LED on and off at will, while the other was to extend the temperature and humidity sensor that is mounted on the micro servo at will. We were also able to set up the buzzer, so that if a reading from the temperature or gas sensor was read at a level deemed “too dangerous” then the buzzer would sound off and alert the hero. Also, the OLED, when first booting up, displays our team logo, and then afterwards displays the current readings from the sensors. Among the issues in the coding, wrong labels and misspellings were the most common. After searching through the code and fixing small errors in typing, or finding keywords that did not match, the code ran through and was able to carry out. Issues with libraries were also something we faced. As certain functions were not integrating into the code well, the whole idea was ultimately scrapped. As for circuits, placing wires into the wrong holes in the breadboard were common, however after looking again for a second time, the mistakes were eventually found and fixed until everything ran smoothly.

CAD Breakdown

In the CAD process, the initial idea was to have a two-part gauntlet to allow for more wrist mobility. The first part is a wrist guard to hold the breadboard and battery. The second part being an enclosure for the hand. On the hand, the palm side holds the ring LED and hazardous gas sensor, while the back of the hand holds the rest of the sensors including the temperature and humidity sensor that is mounted on the micro servo. The initial idea was to individually encase all of our sensors, breadboard and battery in boxes on the gauntlet itself, and enclose them with lids that had snap fit hinges. While the first iteration of the gauntlet had minor hiccups, only one side of the wrist guard needed to be reprinted because it had accidentally merged with the other when printing. Other than that the body itself came out fine. The enclosures for the sensors was a different story. As time kept going and multiple iterations of the tes6bt print hinges failed, the idea was scrapped as a whole in favor of something more time efficient and reliable. The next idea was to enclose our breadboard and battery in a laser cut box that was glued together and mounted on the wrist guard. The box itself went through multiple iterations as the dimensions of the wires were not initially taken into account, nor the room needed for the wire connecting the ESP32 to the portable battery. In the second laser cut, the box had a greater height, and specific cutouts were put in place to allow the wires to go where they needed. On the sides of the box, cutouts were also included for the straps of the velcro to further secure it all together. On the hand itself, the snap hinges were also not going to work out in this case scenario. So the next best idea was to drill into the hand and secure the pieces of equipment that way. This means that on the hand, each sensor is flush with the outside. On the underside, the wires are secured in place with electrical tape and fed through the holes made into the hand itself.

Encounters/Solutions

In our time working on the gauntlet, many problems ensued. Some of which were environmental. Above our work table are leaky pipes and as water droplets kept falling, one of them landed in our breadboard and short circuited it. When the sensors were not reading correctly, at first we thought maybe it was an issue of wiring or maybe even code. However after double checking the circuit itself and reviewing the code, there was nothing as to why everything stopped working all of a sudden. After going through the checklist and then remembering what had happened with the water earlier, the issue became clear that we needed to rewire everything onto a different breadboard. Then we were able to test run our components and make sure that again, everything was running as it should.
